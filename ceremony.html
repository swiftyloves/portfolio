Introduction and Problem Statement

Our team designed the mobile app Ceremony for our client, Tom Meloche. Tom is currently writing a book, Ceremony, which he hopes will improve productivity in the workplace. He will be consulting companies on his book and wanted to build a companion app to help companies implement the book's teachings. He contracted our team to create an interactive prototype to hand off to his developer. The app is expected in the Apple and Android app stores this Spring.

The goal of the app is to empower employees to rate the success of reoccurring meetings ("ceremonies") at work. The app then provides data that will help organization's improve their meetings productivity.

Design Process

We designed this application using the following steps:

  • Frame the problem with user research and an affinity diagram
  • Understand the problem through storyboards, personas, and comparative analysis
  • Explore the solution space with wireframes
  • Iterate based on feedback
  • Create a hi-fi prototype to deliver to the client

User Research

We conducted a user study, in which we interviewed 8 people about their meeting experiences. The interviewees work in a wide range of industries including academic, religious, financial, construction, co-op housing, consulting, and technology. We created an interview protocol and modified it slightly according to different work roles. We intended to discover the common problems among many different situation meetings and to understand the problems throughly.

Storyboards and Personas

The storyboard showed the scenario of the ceremony app usage. First the host could create a ceremony. After the meeting the participants would receive notification and take the survey. The host could see the statistic result after the survey. This storyboard was slightly different from the final design because during this step the design has not yet iterated.

From our interviews we learned about the meetings our interviewees attend regularly. Based on the interviews we two personas: host and participant.

Affinity Diagram

We extracted top-three major findings from each interview and put them on post-it notes. These notes are grouped to create an affinity diagram to understand what attributes apply to a productive meeting.

The key findings from our affinity wall were:

  • Participant engagement in the meeting is key
  • People should feel encouraged to contribute
  • Good leadership matters
  • Good meeting structure increases productivity
  • Positive group culture helps meetings evolve

Designing the Solution

We took a parallel prototyping approach to our design. As a team we discussed and decided the required functions of the application according to both our findings and the client’s requirements. Then every designer worked on the wireframes, designing the features of the application according to their thoughts. We then came together and provided feedback.

We used this technique throughout the entire design process and created a system map, wireframes, lo-fi, and hi-fi mockups with interactions.

Site map

From our affinity wall, we realized the importance of separating roles (i.e. hosts v.s. participants) and how they may use this app differently. However, we wanted to keep one interface for all roles. Hence, we outlined the different ways a user will interact with the app based on their role using a system map. Hi-fi Mockup

Below are mockups representing the main user journey for Ceremony. A host will create a ceremony that coincides with a meeting and invite participants to that ceremony. After a meeting ends everyone will receive a push notification asking for feedback about the meeting. Everyone will provide their anonymous feedback and then analyze the data.

User flow:

  • 1) Set up a ceremony
  • 2) Receive notifications after a ceremony ends
  • 3) Provide anonymous feedback
  • 4) Analyze ceremony data.

Iteration Process: Our design philosophy is that the survey process should be simple and easy for participants to use. because this is a In the step of working on mockup in parallel, we split the steps of creating a ceremony. I was responsible of inviting participants, in which I tried different kinds of designing the progress bar and thought of the steps back and forth. That is, how to connect my design with other pages my team members are responsible.

I decided to tried and design all steps and came out that placing all steps of creating a ceremony in one page would be simpler and easier for users. When we came back to give critique, our team discussed and agreed that this is a better design for creating a ceremony.

Receive notifications and Provide feedback

From our user tests we learned this was the most important part of the user flow. We had to make this as quick and as easy as possible or else users would not respond to the survey prompt. Imagine if you have 10 meetings a week in the app and if each survey takes 5 minutes to complete. That's almost an hour spent every week responding to surveys. People don't have time for that. So we simplified the three questions our client outlined into one overarching "How did the ceremony go?".

A user just needs to tap a smiley button and they are done. However, to allow for more qualitative responses we added attribute buttons that are optional along with an optional comments field. The attributes can be customized. We used our affinity wall findings to create the stock buttons content.

Analyze data

The next step we split the work and I was in charge of presenting the statistic result of surveys. I studied how to present the data visualization to users, received feedbacks of the team members, and iterated. The design iterated with the decisions of different factors, such as changing three questions to one question. Below are the design process of data visualization.

Version 1:

I explored different methods: pie chart, scores, and line charts, as well as managing the time period interaction.

Version 2:

I further deliberated on the interaction of switching questions and date.

Version 3:

The three questions were changed to one question. Thy way of how users take survey were modified from the score range to narrative options. Here we used likert chart to better reveal the information in a time period. In addition I also explored pie chart visualization to show the daily data.

Version 4:

We had debates on the neutral phase. That is, does this option helped the ceremony to evolve? The purpose of the application is to receive feedbacks on how to make the ceremony getting better and to encourage participants to state their opinions. Therefore, we removed the neutral option and forced participants to choose one side. In addition, the size of the face icon also indicated the percentage of the users opinions.

Challenges and Reflection

The challenge for me was to communicate with other designers as well as defended my opinions. The leading style of our team lead is pretty casual. He coordinated the team with everyone’s opinions. Sometimes that would result in a little chaos. Gradually I learnt that if I felt something was important I should just bring it out or even volunteer on doing it. In the process I felt data visualization should be done although we did have enough time. Thus I volunteered to work on it and brought more than my team members expected. I also learnt that there is no standard way to work on a project. We could elaborate as much details as we want but we still need to considerate the limit time resource.
