The Client

Our client, Tom Meloche, is currently writing a book, Ceremony, which he hopes will improve productivity in the workplace. He will be consulting companies on his book and wanted to build a companion app to help companies implement the book's teachings. He contracted our team to create an interactive prototype to hand off to his developer.

It is clear that he had great vision and passion for this project in that he provided us much more than just an idea: he included envisioned features, wireframes, storyboards...etc. While our team could taken his materials and directly started hi-fi prototyping, we found a lack of users' voice/research from the materials Tom provided. Hence, our team decided to still start from the beginning.

User Research

User Interview

We conducted a user study, in which we interviewed 8 people about their meeting experiences. The interviewees work in a wide range of industries including academic, religious, financial, construction, co-op housing, consulting, and technology. We created an interview protocol and modified it slightly according to different work roles. We intended to discover the common problems among many different situation meetings and to understand the problems throughly.

Storyboards and Personas

The storyboard showed the scenario of the ceremony app usage. First the host could create a ceremony. After the meeting the participants would receive notification and take the survey. The host could see the statistic result after the survey. This storyboard was slightly different from the final design because during this step the design has not yet iterated.

From our interviews we learned about the meetings our interviewees attend regularly. Based on the interviews we two personas: host and participant.

Affinity Diagram

We extracted top-three major findings from each interview and put them on post-it notes. These notes are grouped to create an affinity diagram to understand what attributes apply to a productive meeting.

The key findings from our affinity wall were:

Participant engagement is key
Good leadership matters
Participant should feel encouraged to contribute
Good meeting structure increases productivity
Positive group culture helps ceremonies evolve

Ideation

We took a parallel prototyping approach to our design. As a team we discussed and decided the required functions of the application according to both our findings and the clientâ€™s requirements. Then every designer worked on the wireframes, designing the features of the application according to their thoughts. We then came together and provided feedback.

We used this technique throughout the entire design process and created a system map, wireframes, lo-fi, and hi-fi mockups with interactions.

System Site Map

From our affinity wall, we realized the importance of separating roles (i.e. hosts v.s. participants) and how they may use this app differently. However, we wanted to keep one interface for all roles. Hence, we outlined the different ways a user will interact with the app based on their role using a system map. This helped us create our product requirements.

Challenges and Reflection

The first lesson is how to resolve differences in client's view and users' findings. The challenge for me was to communicate with other designers as well as defend my opinions. The leading style of our team lead is pretty casual. He coordinated the team with everyoneâ€™s opinions. Sometimes that would result in a little chaos. Gradually I learnt that if I felt something was important I should just bring it out or even volunteer on doing it. In the process I felt data visualization should be done although we did have enough time. Thus I volunteered to work on it and brought more than my team members expected. I also learnt that there is no standard way to work on a project. We could elaborate as much details as we want but we still need to considerate the limit time resource.
